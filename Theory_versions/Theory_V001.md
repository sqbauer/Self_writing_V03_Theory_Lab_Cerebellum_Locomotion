# Cerebellar Predictive Control of Locomotion

## Abstract

The cerebellum contributes critically to adaptive locomotion, but the mechanism by which it does so remains debated. A plausible working theory is that the cerebellum supports predictive control by learning context-dependent sensorimotor mappings that reduce future locomotor error before it fully emerges. In this framework, granule cells encode high-dimensional locomotor context, Purkinje cells learn predictive associations, and cerebellar output pathways implement bidirectional correction through functionally opposed microzones. Multi-site plasticity may allow this system to operate across both fast and slow adaptation timescales. This theory explains why cerebellar damage impairs feedforward locomotor adaptation more than reactive correction. The main unresolved question is whether these computations are best described as internal models, associative mappings, or two descriptions of the same underlying circuit process.

## Core Claims

1. The cerebellum is more important for predictive feedforward locomotor adaptation than for immediate reactive correction.
2. Granule cells provide context representations that allow predictive locomotor control.
3. Opposed cerebellar microzones may implement bidirectional correction of locomotor errors.
4. Multi-site plasticity may underlie adaptation across multiple timescales.

## Mechanistic Framework

Locomotion requires the nervous system to solve a prediction problem. Each step changes body state, limb dynamics, and sensory inflow. A purely reactive controller would correct only after an error occurs, which is often too late for smooth and stable movement. The cerebellum may solve this by learning to predict the consequences of ongoing locomotor commands and adjusting output in advance.

Within this working model, mossy fiber inputs carry information about sensory context, ongoing movement, and motor commands. Granule cells expand this information into a high-dimensional representation of locomotor state. Purkinje cells then learn associations between these states and future error-related teaching signals carried by climbing fibers. Cerebellar nuclei transform Purkinje cell population activity into corrective motor output.

A key idea in this theory is that functionally opposed microzones may implement bidirectional control. Some microzones may increase corrections in one direction, while others may support the opposite sign of correction. This could provide a circuit-level mechanism for push-pull adjustment of locomotor parameters such as step height, timing, or limb trajectory.

Plasticity is unlikely to reside at a single synapse class. Instead, fast adaptation may depend more strongly on cortical mechanisms, whereas slower retention or recalibration may require plasticity in cerebellar nuclei and related downstream circuits. This would explain why locomotor adaptation often shows multiple timescales.

## Predictions

1. Disrupting cerebellar output should impair feedforward adaptation more strongly than immediate reactive correction.
2. Granule cell population activity should reflect locomotor context more strongly than single movement variables alone.
3. Opposed Purkinje cell populations or microzones should show correction signals of opposite sign during adaptive locomotion.
4. Different forms of cerebellar plasticity should contribute differently to acquisition versus retention of locomotor adaptation.

## Competing Interpretations

This framework overlaps with both internal model theory and associative learning theory. The present version does not yet decide whether these are genuinely different explanations or different levels of description. That remains an open issue.

## Weak Points

1. The role of climbing fibers in natural locomotor adaptation is still underspecified here.
2. The theory remains too qualitative and does not yet specify which locomotor variables are predicted.
3. The relation between cerebellar computations and spinal pattern-generating circuits is not yet well integrated.
4. The theory does not yet clearly distinguish between balance control, limb coordination, and error-driven adaptation.

## Decisive Experiment

Simultaneously record and perturb identified Purkinje cell populations or microzones during split-belt locomotor adaptation while quantifying both predictive aftereffects and immediate reactive corrections. If opposed microzones contribute to bidirectional predictive correction, they should show opponent activity patterns and perturbation should selectively disrupt feedforward adaptation rather than all locomotor correction.
